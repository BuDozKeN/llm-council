# ANN FRIEDMAN WRITING PATTERNS
## Extracted from Actual Published Work

**Purpose:** This document captures Ann Friedman's replicable writing mechanics based on analysis of her published essays, interviews, and op-eds. Use these patterns to write in her style.

---

## CORE STRUCTURAL PATTERN

### Opening (First 1-3 sentences)

**Always start with the interesting observation, claim, or moment.**

**Her patterns:**

1. **Categorical statement:**
   - "You only have to care about one of three things to be a fan of Jonathan Gold's work: good food, great writing, or the city of Los Angeles."
   - "We're in a difficult moment for stuff."

2. **News hook with immediate analysis:**
   - "When Forbes put Kylie Jenner on the cover of its latest issue and declared her one of the '60 richest self-made women,' the magazine seemed to think it was telling a simple, inspiring tale..."

3. **Scene-setting that implies larger point:**
   - (From interview) Jumps right into conversation

**Never:**
- "In today's world..."
- "I've been thinking about..."
- "This is a story about..."
- Any preamble or warm-up

**The test:** Could you delete the first sentence and the piece would be weaker? If no, delete it.

---

### Building (Middle Section)

**Stack specifics. Build through accumulation of concrete details.**

**Her method:**
1. Make initial observation/claim
2. Give 2-5 specific examples
3. Let them accumulate weight
4. State what they mean (the insight)

**Example from Estate Sales:**
- Opens: "We're in a difficult moment for stuff."
- Positions: "I'm not anti-Kondo, but you can put me down as a firm skeptic."
- Explains her view: What stuff means
- Pivots: "Which is why I love estate sales."
- Describes estate sales generally
- Gives ONE detailed example (Joe & Stanley)
- Discovery/climax (memorial booklet)
- Research extension (Stanley's memoir)
- Returns to opening theme with new depth

**Example from Jenner op-ed:**
- News hook
- Two ways to read it
- Explains Forbes scoring system
- Gives specific examples (Zuckerberg, Jenner)
- Backlash examples
- Defense examples
- Uses metaphor (baseball)
- States the real problem
- Ends with solution

**Pattern: Observation → Examples → Insight → More examples → Deeper insight → Conclusion**

---

### Connecting (Where She Makes Her Point)

**State things directly. No hedging.**

**Her style:**
- "I believe that the physical things you collect as you move through your life... add up to something more than their individual utility or aesthetic appeal or heirloom potential."
- "They aren't just things, they're your things."
- "The truth is, in America today it's easy to get from third to home, but it's become almost impossible to get off first base."

**Not:**
- "It could be argued that..."
- "Some might say..."
- "In a sense..."

**When she's certain, she's direct. When she's uncertain, she admits it:**
- "Maybe they weren't lonely at all. Maybe you're just projecting."
- "Usually, you never find out."

---

### Ending

**Never summarize. Always:**
1. Return to opening theme with new depth
2. State implication
3. Make definitive statement
4. Show forward motion

**Examples:**

**Estate Sales ending:**
Returns to "stuff" theme but now weighted with Joe & Stanley's story. Ends with: "And when it does, I hope there is someone standing guard. And someone like me considering the merchandise, trying to figure out the story of how they lived, and taking a piece of that story home to become part of her own."

**Jenner op-ed ending:**
"Media coverage of the most successful ought to reflect that."
(Definitive. Prescriptive. No summary.)

**Never:**
- "In conclusion..."
- "To sum up..."
- "As I've shown..."

## SENTENCE MECHANICS (CRITICAL)

### Wild Length Variation

**This is her signature move. Extreme variation in single paragraphs.**

**Example from Estate Sales (one paragraph):**
- 13 words: "I'm not anti-Kondo, but you can put me down as a firm skeptic."
- 46 words: "I believe that the physical things you collect as you move through your life — even those that don't make your stomach flip with joy — add up to something more than their individual utility or aesthetic appeal or heirloom potential."
- 7 words: "They aren't just things, they're your things."
- 20 words: "And if you remove yourself from the picture, the stuff you surround yourself with tells a story about you."
- 9 words: "It is a physical autobiography you write by living."

**Pattern: 13, 46, 7, 20, 9 words**

**Another example (Jenner):**
- 54 words: "When Forbes put Kylie Jenner on the cover of its latest issue and declared her one of the '60 richest self-made women,' the magazine seemed to think it was telling a simple, inspiring tale: About how the youngest child of America's reality-TV royal family, the Kardashians, became an almost-billionaire cosmetics mogul before the age of 21."
- 12 words: "But the meaning of the story lay in the politics of the beholder."

**Pattern: 54, 12 words**

**How to replicate:**
- Short declarative sentence (5-10 words)
- Long sentence with clauses/details (30-50 words)
- Fragment or very short (2-7 words)
- Medium build (15-25 words)
- Short or fragment again

**Never have uniform sentence length. Ever.**

---

### Parenthetical Thinking

**Show your thought process using parentheses.**

**Her usage:**
- "(Are liontamers real? I just made this up.)"
- "(You can't make this up)"
- "(In some cases, when she knows the offender, she unsubscribes them from her list before she shames them on it. I can't decide if that's kind or if it's essentially talking behind someone's back.)"

**Purpose:**
- Adds conversational tone
- Shows uncertainty or aside
- Self-aware commentary
- Personal reaction

**Use for:**
- Admitting you made something up
- Wondering about something
- Quick tangent
- Self-aware commentary

---

### Em Dashes for Expansion

**Use em dashes to add information, interrupt yourself, or create dramatic pause.**

**Her usage:**
- "I believe that the physical things you collect as you move through your life — even those that don't make your stomach flip with joy — add up to something more..."
- "The estate sale, unlike its close relative the yard sale, is not a selective culling of possessions. It's a going-out-of-business event for one person's life. 'Full House Sale — 60 Years accumulation,' boasts a local listing..."

**Pattern:**
- Main clause — expansion/interruption — continuation
- Creates rhythm
- Adds information without new sentence
- More dramatic than commas

---

### Questions

**Ask questions to yourself and the reader. Then answer them or leave them hanging.**

**Her style:**
- "Do these things spark joy? Is the mundane supposed to spark joy?"
- "Somehow, I don't think that's the point."

(Question-question-answer pattern)

- "Have you been there?"
- "No. I would love to go."

(Question in dialogue, natural response)

**Use questions to:**
- Pivot thinking
- Challenge assumptions
- Engage reader
- Set up your point

**Don't:**
- Ask questions you don't address
- Use rhetorical questions as crutch
- Ask obvious questions

---

## PARAGRAPH STRUCTURE

### Single-Sentence Paragraphs

**Use for emphasis. Often.**

**Her usage:**
- "Which is why I love estate sales."
- "Usually, you never find out."
- "Aha! So our clothes horse — Joe or Stanley — was a gay man."
- "Somehow, I don't think that's the point."

**When to use:**
- After building tension
- For revelation/discovery
- To make definitive point
- For dramatic pause
- To transition

**Can be fragment:**
- "Usually, you never find out."
- "Aha!"

---

### Paragraph Length Variation

**Like sentences, vary wildly.**

**Her patterns:**
- 1 sentence (emphasis)
- 2-4 sentences (standard building)
- 6-10 sentences (narrative/detailed explanation)
- Back to 1 sentence

**Example from Estate Sales:**
```
[1 sentence]
"Which is why I love estate sales."

[4 sentences]
"The estate sale, unlike its close relative the yard sale, is not a selective culling of possessions. It's a going-out-of-business event for one person's life. 'Full House Sale — 60 Years accumulation,' boasts a local listing on EstateSales.net..."

[8 sentences - long detailed paragraph describing walking through estate sale]
"You often enter an estate sale through the garage..."

[1 sentence]
"The jewelry and silver is usually on a table near the checkout..."
```

**Never:**
- All paragraphs same length
- Wall of text
- Every sentence its own paragraph

---

## VOICE CHARACTERISTICS

### First Person

**Use "I" liberally. She's present in her writing.**

**Her usage:**
- "I love estate sales"
- "I believe that..."
- "I wondered if..."
- "I decided to Google him"
- "I'm not anti-Kondo"
- "I tucked one of the booklets into my bag"

**When she uses it:**
- Stating opinions ("I believe")
- Describing actions ("I decided")
- Positioning herself ("I'm not anti-Kondo")
- Personal experience as evidence

**Not performative. Natural.**

---

### Second Person

**Use "you" to make observations universal.**

**Her usage:**
- "You often enter an estate sale through the garage."
- "You swear sometimes that you can feel the loneliness of former inhabitants."
- "You're the centrifuge holding all of this stuff together."
- "If you're paying attention, you can put together a story about who they were."

**Purpose:**
- Bring reader into experience
- Universal observation
- Instructional
- Shared understanding

---

### Direct Statements

**When certain, be certain. No hedging.**

**Her certainty:**
- "They aren't just things, they're your things."
- "The truth is, in America today it's easy to get from third to home, but it's become almost impossible to get off first base."
- "Individually, the things you own are just things."

**Not:**
- "It seems that things might not be just things"
- "One could argue that..."
- "In a way, things are perhaps..."

**But when uncertain, admit it:**
- "Maybe they weren't lonely at all. Maybe you're just projecting."
- "Usually, you never find out."
- "I wondered if..."

**The key:** Know when you're certain vs. uncertain. Write accordingly.

---

## CONNECTING DEVICES

### "Which is why..."

**This is her favorite causal connector.**

**Her usage:**
- "Which is why I love estate sales."
- "Which is why estate sales are the perfect hobby for people who like to make up stories."

**Use when:**
- Connecting observation to conclusion
- Showing causality
- Moving from setup to payoff

**Alternative forms:**
- "This is why..."
- "And that's why..."

---

### "And then..."

**For narrative progression.**

**Her usage:**
- "And then, depending on the sticker price, you can approach the till..."
- "And then I was hooked." (Gold interview, quoting him)

---

### "But..."

**For contrast or pivot. Often starts sentences.**

**Her usage:**
- "But the meaning of the story lay in the politics of the beholder."
- "But what makes them special is that they're yours."
- "But on a bookcase in the hallway, I found the holy grail..."

**Starting sentences with "But" is fine. She does it constantly.**

---

### Often No Transition

**Just move. Don't over-signal.**

**She frequently jumps between ideas without explicit connectors.**

Example paragraph transitions:
[Paragraph about Marie Kondo's philosophy]

[No transition]

[Next paragraph: "Which is why I love estate sales."]

The connection is implied, not stated.

---

## SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES

### Listing for Accumulation

**Build by stacking specific details.**

**Example (Estate Sales):**
"The mugs they drank coffee from every morning in this kitchen. The chairs they pushed into the soft sand of the beach every summer. The books they read repeatedly, and the books they kept on these shelves because they always meant to read them but never got around to it. The framed prints that faded based on how the sun hit them every afternoon in this den."

**Pattern:**
- Specific item + specific use/detail
- Specific item + specific use/detail
- Specific item + specific use/detail
- Repeat until weight accumulates

**Creates:** Texture, specificity, emotional weight

---

### Conversational Asides

**Comment on your own writing as you write.**

**Her moves:**
- "You can't make this up"
- "I regret not trying it on"
- "(Are liontamers real? I just made this up.)"
- "As should you be!"

**Shows:**
- Self-awareness
- Humor
- Personal reaction
- That she's a person writing, not a voice from nowhere

---

### Admitting What You Don't Know

**Uncertainty is humanizing.**

**Her style:**
- "Maybe they weren't lonely at all. Maybe you're just projecting."
- "Usually, you never find out."
- "I wondered if the portrait was sitting there because he was estranged from the son and ex-wife."

**Not:**
- Making up facts
- Pretending certainty
- Hedging everything
- Being wishy-washy

**Be certain when certain. Admit uncertainty when uncertain.**

---

### Specificity as Evidence

**Concrete details do the work.**

**Not:** "The house had men's clothing."

**Instead:** "The bedroom had three closets packed with menswear — extra-long and -large button-up shirts in every imaginable pattern."

**Not:** "There were religious books."

**Instead:** "In the den, which was ringed with bookshelves, we noticed an abundance of old-Hollywood biographies, art books, Methodist texts, and a book about closeted gay men that was stored — you can't make this up — on a shelf in a closet."

**Every detail serves the story she's building.**

---

### Personal Experience as Cultural Argument

**Use your life to make larger points.**

**Her structure:**
1. Cultural observation (stuff, wealth, food culture)
2. Personal positioning ("I'm not anti-Kondo")
3. Personal experience as evidence (estate sale)
4. Return to cultural point with new depth

**The personal narrative IS the argument.**

Not: "I think this, here's why [abstract reasoning]"

Instead: "I think this, here's what happened to me [concrete story], which shows why"

## DIALOGUE STYLE

### From Gold Interview

**Natural conversation. Capturing speech patterns.**

**Her technique:**
- Minimal tags ("JG:" "AF:")
- Includes interruptions: "David Chang Instagrammed it —"
- Captures vocal patterns: "[Laughs.]" "[Laughs heartily.]"
- Incomplete thoughts: "So I ate at Sun Nong Dan, but I also ate at three other places that did that dish."
- Natural back-and-forth

**Shows:**
- Real conversation, not cleaned-up interview
- Personality through speech
- Rapport

---

## POSITIONING HERSELF

### The "I'm not X, but..." Move

**Pattern: Acknowledge the popular thing, position against it moderately.**

**Her exact words:**
"I'm not anti-Kondo, but you can put me down as a firm skeptic."

**Structure:**
- "I'm not [extreme position]"
- "but [my actual position which is more moderate/nuanced]"

**Purpose:**
- Shows she's considered the popular view
- Not dismissive
- But has her own take
- Invites reader along

**Use when:** You have a contrarian view but don't want to seem like you're just being contrary.

## HUMOR & LIGHTNESS

### Self-Deprecating

**She makes fun of herself.**

**Examples:**
- "(Are liontamers real? I just made this up.)"
- "Sorry, I need this part today." [after someone mentions cow parts]
- "I regret not trying it on." [about the sequined dress]

---

### Observational Comedy

**Noticing absurdities.**

**Examples:**
- The woman with blowtorch melting cheese on already-bubbling dish
- "Stanley Stanley Stanley" belt
- Lube bottle in estate sale bathroom
- The "I said NO pen" Cynthia uses

**Her tone:** Amused observer, not mean comedian

---

### Restraint

**She could make more jokes but doesn't.**

**Shows humor through:**
- Selection of details
- Juxtaposition
- Understatement
- One-line observations

**Not through:**
- Trying to be funny constantly
- Forced jokes
- Setup-punchline structure

---

## SENTENCE STARTERS SHE USES

**And** - constantly, starts sentences
**But** - for contrast, starts sentences
**So** - for causality
**Which is why** - her favorite
**Maybe** - when uncertain
**There's** - descriptive
**I** - personal experience
**You** - universal observation
**This** - referring to previous point

**She does NOT use:**
- "In today's world"
- "Furthermore"
- "Moreover"
- "In conclusion"
- "Additionally"
- "However" (uses "But" instead)

---

## WHAT SHE NEVER DOES

**Corporate language:**
- "Leverage"
- "Utilize"
- "Facilitate"
- "Implement"
- "Synergize"

**Academic hedging:**
- "It could be argued that"
- "One might say"
- "In a sense"
- "To some extent"
- "Relatively speaking"

**Throat-clearing:**
- "Before I begin"
- "First, let me say"
- "I want to talk about"
- "This essay will explore"

**Over-explaining:**
- Defining common terms
- Explaining why she's qualified
- Justifying her opinions before stating them

**Summaries:**
- "In conclusion"
- "To sum up"
- "As I've shown"
- Ending with recap

## THE ANN FRIEDMAN CHECKLIST

**Before publishing, verify:**

**Opening:**
- [ ] Starts with interesting observation/claim/moment?
- [ ] Zero throat-clearing?
- [ ] Positions her viewpoint within first 3 paragraphs?

**Sentences:**
- [ ] WILD length variation (some 5 words, some 50 words)?
- [ ] Mix of declarative, questions, fragments?
- [ ] Em dashes used for expansion?
- [ ] Parenthetical asides included?

**Paragraphs:**
- [ ] Some single-sentence paragraphs?
- [ ] Varied length (1, 3, 8, 2, 1, etc)?
- [ ] NOT all same length?

**Voice:**
- [ ] First person when relevant ("I believe", "I wondered")?
- [ ] Second person for universal observations?
- [ ] Direct statements when certain?
- [ ] Admits uncertainty when uncertain?

**Structure:**
- [ ] Builds through specific examples?
- [ ] Uses personal experience as evidence?
- [ ] Returns to opening theme with depth?

**Ending:**
- [ ] No summary?
- [ ] Forward motion or implication?
- [ ] Definitive statement or question?

**Overall:**
- [ ] Sounds like a smart person talking, not writing?
- [ ] Humor through observation, not performance?
- [ ] Concrete details, not abstractions?
- [ ] Could appear in a real publication?

## THE ULTIMATE TEST

**Read it aloud.**

Does it sound like someone talking to you over coffee? Someone who:
- Knows their stuff
- Doesn't need to prove it
- Has specific stories
- Thinks while talking
- Sometimes interrupts themselves
- Admits what they don't know
- Makes definitive points
- Uses humor lightly
- Varies their rhythm
- Sounds like Ann Friedman

If YES → you've got it

If NO → probably too stiff, too uniform, too hedged, or too performed

## FINAL NOTES

**Ann Friedman's style is:**
- Conversational but not casual
- Personal but not confessional
- Specific but not technical
- Smart but not academic
- Funny but not jokey
- Direct but not blunt
- Certain when warranted
- Uncertain when honest

**The key is variation:**
- Sentence length
- Paragraph length
- Tone (serious to light)
- Certainty level
- POV (I, you, they)

**And accumulation:**
- Build through specifics
- Let details create meaning
- Stack examples
- Return to themes

**This is not a template. These are patterns to internalize and deploy naturally based on what each piece needs.**
